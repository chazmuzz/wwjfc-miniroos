# WWJFC MiniRoos Programme

This page is the main reference for how the Warilla Wanderers Junior Football Club MiniRoos programme is intended to run. It sets out the purpose of the programme, how sessions should feel, and what expectations should be clear for players, parents, coaches and volunteers.

At WWJFC, MiniRoos currently covers `U6` and `U7`.

- Players born in `2021` or `2020` are in `U6`.
- Players born in `2019` are in `U7`.

The programme is designed as a shared framework across both age groups. Coaches work together, sessions are planned with flexibility, and the focus stays on giving children a strong early football experience.

## Core objectives

- Create a fun, safe and welcoming first football experience.
- Build strong engagement so kids want to keep coming back.
- Develop basic football skills through repetition, games and lots of touches on the ball.
- Establish good habits around participation, equipment, listening and sportsmanship.
- Create a clear development pathway that can support future age groups.
- Target around 50 players across the total cohort.
- Target 50% female participation across players, coaches and volunteers.

## Programme model

- One MiniRoos framework across `U6` and `U7` rather than isolated teams working separately.
- Shared programme led collaboratively by coaches.
- Players are part of one overall cohort, with Saturday squads used for organisation rather than as fixed-status teams.
- Squads can change over time to support social mixing, flexibility and equal belonging across the programme.
- Parent volunteers may be required to assist with training drills.
- Coaches are encouraged to be football-minded and to progress toward the Foundation of Football course.
- Training takes place during school term only.
- Official competitions may not begin until around Easter, but the programme can begin earlier and build week by week.

## Capacity and structure

- Total registered numbers are expected to be around `50` players across `U6` and `U7`.
- A practical working model is around `10` squads of `5` players on Saturdays, adjusted as needed for attendance.
- This scale supports energetic sessions, multiple small-sided games, social connection across the cohort, and flexibility when families are absent.
- The aim is to run the age groups as a programme rather than a collection of isolated teams.

## Attendance assumptions

- Actual attendance on any given day will likely be lower than total registrations.
- Early in the season, attendance may sit at 90% or higher.
- As the season progresses, attendance may drop closer to around 60% on some days.
- Session design should be flexible enough to cope with both high-attendance and lower-attendance days.

## Weekly schedule and location

- Training session: Wednesday, `4:30pm` to `5:30pm`.
- Training starts on Wednesday `25 March`.
- There will be a break from training during the winter school holidays.
- Additional sessions can be added later if needed.
- Players are encouraged to attend the weekly training session where possible.
- Saturday game attendance, approximately `8:00am` to `10:00am`, is a firmer expectation.
- Location: Morley Park, Warilla.
- Best parking access: off Trumper Road.

## Match day approach

- Saturday squads are used to allocate pitches, manage substitutions and keep the morning organised.
- Squads should not be treated as permanent teams or as markers of status within the programme.
- Small-sided football should be prioritised wherever possible to maximise involvement.
- Rolling substitutions should be used to support equal game time and keep players engaged.
- Squad groupings can change during the season to support flexibility, fairness and social mixing.

## Player development philosophy

Player skill development sits at the centre of the MiniRoos programme. At this age, the most important thing is not results, positions or tactics. It is helping children become comfortable with the ball, confident in movement, and enthusiastic about playing.

Sessions should maximise touches, encourage creativity, and build simple physical literacy such as balance, coordination, running, stopping, turning and reacting.

Because the cohort includes both `U6` and `U7` players, activities should have simple scaling options so the same session structure can work for children at slightly different stages of development.

## Session environment

- Sessions should be energetic, positive and well organised.
- Activities should minimise standing around and maximise involvement.
- Coaches should use simple language, short instructions and quick demonstrations.
- The environment should support children with different confidence levels and learning speeds.
- A snack break can be built into sessions where needed, especially during colder months.

## Session structure

- A typical session can begin with arrival play, allowing children to settle in with a ball and minimal instruction.
- Ball mastery or station-based activities should follow, with a high ball-per-player ratio and simple variations.
- Small-sided games such as `1v1`, `2v2` and `3v3` should be used regularly to build decision-making and confidence.
- Sessions can finish with game-like activity or mini matches, followed by a brief cool-down and positive reflection.
- The exact mix can change week to week, but the rhythm should stay familiar enough that players know what to expect.

## Coaching and delivery

- Coaches should work as one coaching group rather than as isolated team coaches.
- Session ideas, plans and observations should be shared openly so delivery stays consistent across the programme.
- Responsibility should be spread across the coaching group to reduce burnout and make the programme sustainable.
- A practical target is roughly `1` coach for every `8` to `10` players.
- Parent volunteers can support stations, transitions and player engagement under the direction of coaches.

## Equipment and safety

- Shin pads must be worn for every session.
- Boots must be worn for every session.
- Long socks must be worn for every session.
- Players should bring a correctly sized football for home practice, ideally size `2` or size `3`, and not bigger.
- Piercings should be removed where possible. If they cannot be removed, they should be taped safely.

## Parent support

Parents play a major role in helping children enjoy and stick with football. Useful support includes:

- Encouraging regular attendance.
- Helping children arrive with the right gear.
- Keeping expectations realistic, especially as many players will be joining organised football for the first time.
- Supporting effort, bravery, listening and sportsmanship rather than only goals or standout performance.
- Avoiding sideline coaching and allowing children to make mistakes and solve simple problems for themselves.
- Celebrating improvement, courage and persistence, not just visible success.
- Providing encouragement to practise casually at home.
- Steering screen time toward football-related games or content where it helps build a general understanding of the game.

## Recognition and rewards

The programme should avoid traditional player-of-the-game awards if they are heavily performance-based or feel arbitrary. A better approach is to recognise qualities such as:

- Effort
- Courage
- Sportsmanship
- Teamwork
- Listening
- Persistence

## Training activity ideas

1. Ball familiarisation and free dribbling
2. Red light, green light with the ball
3. Gates dribbling
4. Sharks and minnows
5. Simple `1v1` battles
6. Cone turning races
7. Passing through gates
8. Shooting on small goals
9. Numbers game or reaction game
10. Small-sided match play

## Practical delivery notes

- Plan every session so it can be adjusted quickly for roughly `30` to `45` attendees.
- Use station-based formats where possible to reduce bottlenecks.
- Have a simpler variation and a harder variation for most activities so `U6` and `U7` players can both be engaged.
- Keep transitions short and the ball-per-player ratio as high as possible.

## Nutrition and wellbeing

- The club does not provide formal nutrition advice as part of the MiniRoos programme.
- Any food or wellbeing messaging should stay light-touch, practical and non-judgemental.
- Snack breaks can be useful for younger players during longer sessions, with families managing individual needs and allergies.

## Success measures

- Children look forward to training and Saturday games.
- Attendance remains strong across the season.
- Players become more confident with the ball and more comfortable participating.
- Parents feel informed, welcomed and included in the programme.
- Players want to return the following season.

## Long-term vision

- Build lifelong enjoyment of football and healthy attitudes toward sport.
- Create a strong club culture that families want to stay connected to.
- Establish a sustainable development pathway that can keep improving over time.
- The goal is not to produce elite players at six or seven, but to help children want to keep playing.

## Guiding principle

The aim is not just to get through one season. The goal is to create a strong starting point for young players and a repeatable club model that can be improved, reused and expanded over time.
