# WWJFC U6 Programme Plan

## Purpose
This document captures the current plan for the WWJFC U6 MiniRoos programme. It is intended to provide a clear framework for how the programme will run, what it is trying to achieve, and what expectations should be set for players, parents, coaches, and volunteers.

The programme is being designed as a framework and system for the U6 age group, rather than as a set of separate teams. The players are one cohort. Coaches work together as a team, sharing ideas and the burden of coaching.

## Core Objectives
- Create a fun, safe and welcoming first football experience.
- Build strong engagement so kids want to keep coming back.
- Develop basic football skills through repetition, games and lots of touches on the ball.
- Establish good habits around participation, equipment, listening and sportsmanship.
- Create a clear development pathway that can support future age groups.
- Target 40 players in the cohort.
- Target 50% female participation across players, coaches and volunteers.

## Programme Model
- One U6 cohort rather than separate teams.
- Shared programme led collaboratively by coaches.
- Parent volunteers may be required to assist with training drills.
- Soft expectation that coaches are already football-minded and intend to eventually complete the Foundation of Football course.
- Training takes place during school term only.
- Official competitions may not begin until around Easter, but the programme can begin earlier and build week by week.

## Weekly Schedule
- Training sessions: Monday and Wednesday, 3:45pm to 5:15pm.
- Soft expectation that each player attends at least one training session per week.
- Firmer expectation around Saturday game attendance, approximately 8:00am to 10:00am.
- Programme start date: Saturday 7 February.

## Player Development Philosophy
Player skill development should sit at the centre of the U6 programme. At this age, the most important thing is not results, positions or tactics. It is helping children become comfortable with the ball, confident in movement, and enthusiastic about playing. Sessions should be designed to maximise touches, encourage creativity, and build simple physical literacy such as balance, coordination, running, stopping, turning and reacting.

## Session Environment
- Sessions should be energetic, positive and well-organised.
- Activities should minimise standing around and maximise involvement.
- Coaches should use simple language, short instructions and quick demonstrations.
- The environment should support children with different confidence levels and learning speeds.
- A snack break can be built into sessions where needed, especially during colder months when the season spans autumn and winter. Families can be encouraged to send practical snacks such as banana, orange slices, berries or similar simple options.

## Equipment and Safety
- Shin pads must be worn for every session.
- Boots must be worn for every session.
- Long socks must be worn for every session.
- Players should bring a correctly sized football for home practice, ideally size 2 or size 3, and not bigger.
- Piercings should be removed where possible. If they cannot be removed, they should be taped safely.

## Parent Support
Parents play a major role in helping children enjoy and stick with football. Useful support includes:
- Encouraging regular attendance.
- Helping children arrive with the right gear.
- Keeping expectations realistic, especially as many players will be joining a football team for the first time and may come up against children who have already completed multiple seasons.
- Supporting effort, bravery, listening and sportsmanship rather than only goals or standout performance.
- Providing encouragement to practise casually at home.
- If children are having screen time, it can be helpful to steer them towards football games, which can be surprisingly useful for developing a general understanding of the game.

## Recognition and Rewards
The programme should avoid traditional “player of the game” awards if they are either heavily performance-based or feel arbitrary. A better approach is to explicitly recognise qualities such as:
- Effort
- Courage
- Sportsmanship
- Teamwork
- Listening
- Persistence

## Training Activity Ideas
1. Ball familiarisation and free dribbling
2. Red light, green light with the ball
3. Gates dribbling
4. Sharks and minnows
5. Simple 1v1 battles
6. Cone turning races
7. Passing through gates
8. Shooting on small goals
9. Numbers game / reaction game
10. Small-sided match play

## FAQ
### Is this programme only about fun?
No. Fun is essential, but the programme also aims to build football ability, confidence and habits that will help players improve over time. At U6, development works best when children enjoy what they are doing and want to return each week.

### Do players need to attend both training sessions every week?
Not necessarily. The expectation is softer around training attendance, with the aim that players attend at least one session per week where possible. Saturday participation is more important for building team rhythm and commitment.

### Do we need parent help?
Yes, in many cases. Parent volunteers can make a big difference by helping set up activities, managing flow between drills, and supporting kids who need extra encouragement.

### Is the club replacing individual parent responsibility?
No. The programme can support families with structure, guidance and a positive football environment, but it is not a replacement for what happens at home. Parent encouragement, routines, transport, nutrition decisions and day-to-day support still matter greatly.

## Guiding Principle
This programme is intended to create a strong starting point for young players and a repeatable system for the club. The goal is not just to get through one season, but to build a model that can be improved, reused and expanded over time.
